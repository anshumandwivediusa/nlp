# TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) — Explained with Examples

## The Core Idea

TF-IDF fixes BoW's biggest flaw: **common words dominate the vector even though they carry little meaning.** Instead of just counting how often a word appears, TF-IDF asks a smarter question:

> *"How important is this word to **this specific document**, relative to how common it is **across all documents**?"*

A word gets a **high TF-IDF score** if it appears frequently in *this* document but rarely in *other* documents — meaning it's likely a distinguishing, informative word for that document. A word gets a **low score** if it's frequent everywhere (like "the", "is", "on") — meaning it's not useful for telling documents apart.

## The Formula

TF-IDF is the product of two components:

$$
\text{TF-IDF}(w, d) = \text{TF}(w, d) \times \text{IDF}(w)
$$

**1. Term Frequency (TF)** — how often word `w` appears in document `d`, normalized by document length:

$$
\text{TF}(w, d) = \frac{\text{count of } w \text{ in } d}{\text{total words in } d}
$$

**2. Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)** — how rare word `w` is across *all* documents:

$$
\text{IDF}(w) = \log\left(\frac{N}{\text{DF}(w)}\right)
$$

Where:
- `N` = total number of documents
- `DF(w)` = number of documents containing word `w`

**Intuition**: if a word appears in *every* document, `DF(w) = N`, so `IDF(w) = log(1) = 0` — meaning that word contributes **nothing** to distinguishing documents. If a word appears in only *one* document, IDF is large — meaning it's highly distinctive.

## Step-by-Step Example (Same Docs as Before)

```
Doc1: "the cat sat on the mat"     (6 words total)
Doc2: "the dog sat on the log"     (6 words total)
```

**Step 1 — Compute TF for each word in Doc1:**

| Word | Count in Doc1 | TF (count / 6) |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| the | 2 | 0.33 |
| cat | 1 | 0.17 |
| sat | 1 | 0.17 |
| on | 1 | 0.17 |
| mat | 1 | 0.17 |

**Step 2 — Compute IDF for each word** (N = 2 documents total):

| Word | Appears in # docs (DF) | IDF = log(N/DF) |
|------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| the | 2 (both docs) | log(2/2) = **0** |
| sat | 2 (both docs) | log(2/2) = **0** |
| on | 2 (both docs) | log(2/2) = **0** |
| cat | 1 (only Doc1) | log(2/1) = **0.301** |
| mat | 1 (only Doc1) | log(2/1) = **0.301** |
| dog | 1 (only Doc2) | log(2/1) = **0.301** |
| log | 1 (only Doc2) | log(2/1) = **0.301** |

**Step 3 — Multiply TF × IDF for Doc1:**

| Word | TF | IDF | TF-IDF |
|------|-----|------|---------|
| the | 0.33 | 0 | **0.000** |
| sat | 0.17 | 0 | **0.000** |
| on | 0.17 | 0 | **0.000** |
| cat | 0.17 | 0.301 | **0.051** |
| mat | 0.17 | 0.301 | **0.051** |

## The Payoff — Compare to Raw BoW

Recall the raw BoW vector for Doc1:

```
BoW:    [cat=1, dog=0, log=0, mat=1, on=1, sat=1, the=2]
                                                    ↑
                                          "the" had the HIGHEST count
```

Now the TF-IDF vector for Doc1:

```
TF-IDF: [cat=0.051, dog=0, log=0, mat=0.051, on=0, sat=0, the=0]
                                                          ↑
                                          "the" is now ZERO — correctly
                                          identified as uninformative
```

This is the entire point of TF-IDF: **"cat" and "mat" — the words that actually make Doc1 distinctive — now stand out**, while "the", "sat", and "on" (which appear in *every* document and don't help distinguish anything) are pushed down to zero or near-zero.

## Why It's ✅ Better Than BoW

- **Down-weights common/filler words** automatically — no manual stopword list needed (though people often still use one alongside it).
- **Highlights distinctive, informative words** — great for tasks like search engines, document ranking, and keyword extraction.
- **Still simple, fast, and interpretable** — no training required, just counting and a log formula.
- **Strong baseline** for text classification and information retrieval — TF-IDF + cosine similarity was the backbone of search engines for decades.

## Why It's ❌ Still Limited

**1. Still no real semantics**
```
TF-IDF("happy") and TF-IDF("joyful") → still completely unrelated dimensions
```
TF-IDF is a smarter *weighting* scheme, but it's still built on exact word matching — it has no idea that "happy" and "joyful" mean similar things.

**2. Still loses word order**
```
"dog bites man" and "man bites dog" → same TF-IDF vector
```
Same problem as BoW — no sequence information is retained.

**3. Still high-dimensional and sparse**
Vector length is still tied to vocabulary size (can be tens of thousands of dimensions), most of which are zero for any given document.

**4. Sensitive to corpus composition**
IDF values depend entirely on what documents happen to be in your dataset. Add or remove documents, and every IDF score shifts — TF-IDF has no fixed, universal notion of a word's importance.

|---|---|---|---|
| Represents | Single word | Whole document | Whole document |
| Captures frequency | ❌ | ✅ | ✅ (weighted) |
| Down-weights common words | ❌ | ❌ | ✅ |
| Captures word order | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ |
